Testchem: Provide customized winding solutions for the composites industry

Category: Company headlines    release time: 2020-01-15    Author：admin
  share to:   
QR code sharing

   A few days ago, Composites in Manufacturing (CIM) magazine conducted an exclusive interview with Luke Vardy, general manager of Cygnet Texkimp, a British composite equipment manufacturer. The interview focused on the Cygnet Texkimp company’s experience in customized winding equipment. The interview text was established as follows:

Filament winding. Developed as early as the late 1940s, it is one of the original composite material manufacturing processes. It is traditionally used to manufacture lightweight and high-performance structural parts, such as pressure vessels, pipelines, ship masts and antenna masts. Wait.

In recent years, as the market has increasingly higher requirements for speed, cost-effectiveness, scale and complexity, people's interest in this process and the products it can develop has continued to increase. Filament winding has many applications, but if it is to be used as a viable manufacturing method, especially in the high-volume automotive industry, it must be faster and more accurate.

Traditional fiber winding is to wind the fiber on a rotating mandrel under tension to obtain the desired shape. Generally, the fiber is impregnated with resin first, and then immediately wound on the mandrel, but now more and more machines can also directly wind prepreg tape, monofilament prepreg tape or dry fiber. Combining this process with robotics technology also makes it more flexible to manufacture components of different sizes and shapes, including small aircraft spars and fuselages, automobile wheels, crash barriers and wind turbines.


The mandrel itself is an important consideration. It can be kept in the process, for example, to help resist shocks when making car parts, or it can be extracted more traditionally, such as when making masts.

Three years ago, we carried out a large-scale research project in cooperation with the University of Manchester, and developed a series of technologies that used robotics and automation technology to break through the limits of filament winding to improve production speed, tension and precision.

The technology produced in this work is our three-dimensional (3D) winding equipment: a winding equipment based on the 9-axis robot winding concept, first developed by Professor Prasad Potluri, aimed at producing complex parts with different cross-sections and shapes. This high-speed multi-head equipment combines rotation and fiber feeding on a mechanical device that automatically moves around a static mandrel and winds in the process. Multiple working heads or spindle positions (each with a yarn roll) are mounted on a rotating ring. The size of the ring and the number of work pieces installed are expandable and depend on the size of the components produced. Our laboratory equipment can hold up to 16 cones, which means it can wind various components, from aerospace fuel pipelines to automobile anti-collision beams, to hydrogen fuel cells and oil and gas pipelines, to wind turbine blades and Aircraft fuselage. By installing multiple yarn feeding mechanisms, we can also speed up the usually relatively slow process. The device can wind carbon fiber at a speed of 1 kg/min, which means it can wind an aircraft spar in a matter of minutes.

A key feature of the three-dimensional winding equipment is that it can handle more complex and curved components with higher precision. The inspiration for developing it came from the air intake pipe of an aircraft. It is a complex curved part with a length of about 3 meters. One end has a large round shape and the other end transitions to a smaller square shape. Three-dimensional winding equipment can match this complexity. Compared with fixed winding machines, mobile winding can provide more degrees of freedom.

We initially thought that the three-dimensional winding equipment is more like a knitting machine, because it is equipped with a large swivel of rotating cones that is reminiscent of weaving technology. But we have found that we can produce a non-crimped material that is stronger and less shearing than woven materials.

The scope of work we have done for the development of three-dimensional winding equipment is very wide, so that we have a lot of knowledge reserves, so that we can further develop two other less complicated filament winding equipment that is equally useful to the market.

One of them is a robot filament winding machine, which retains the traditional filament winding structure, that is, the mandrel rotates, but its position is fixed, and the yarn feeding device is located on the robotic arm, which allows the fiber to be fed from multiple moving axes. Both can be combined with the driven creel to the robot, so that the tension of each spindle position or axial direction can be adjusted individually to control the yarn feeding, and the accuracy and consistency of the finished product can be achieved. This also means that the yarn feeding speed can be changed each time to provide higher resolution to handle smaller-scale features or complex geometric structures.

Another technology launched at JEC in 2019 is a four-axis winding machine. This machine is suitable for a wider market, but still retains our technical expertise, including optimized geometry, reduced number of contact points and correct surface treatment.

Our current research and development focus is to combine filament winding equipment with other equipment in our product library to further expand the capabilities of this technology. We use fiber unwinding creel, automatic loading and unloading, resin mixing, data recording and video quality inspection technology to eliminate the possibility of fiber damage, realize the traceability of the whole process, and meet the industry 4.0 standard.

We are already producing high-speed prepreg and slitting equipment, and now we are able to integrate these technologies with our filament winding technology. We have also begun to provide customers with filament winding test machine services. The thermoplastic prepreg tapes used are produced on the thermoplastic test line of our R&D center. This will enable our customers to manufacture complex parts that work at high temperatures, making them more suitable for aerospace applications.

Facts have proved that our long-term investment in research and development is our advantage in effective competition in the global market. Inviting customers to try our equipment in our ** exhibition halls and development centers can enable us and customers to establish a trusting and sustainable cooperative relationship, and continue to promote this high level of intensive product development.
