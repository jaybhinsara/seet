MIT researchers develop a new method for producing aerospace-grade composite materials without the need for large autoclaves

Category: Company headlines    release time: 2020-03-23    Author：admin
  share to:   
QR code sharing

Researchers at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology have newly developed a method to produce aerospace-grade composite materials without the need for large heating furnaces and autoclaves. New technologies may help speed up the manufacturing of aircraft and other large high-performance composite structures, such as wind turbine blades. The research results are published in "Advanced Materials Interface", the paper details a new method of carbon nanotube film production of aviation-grade composite materials.

Jeonyoo Lee, Postdoctoral Fellow, Massachusetts Institute of Technology 


        Brian Wardle, a professor at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology School of Aeronautics and Astronautics, said: “If you want to manufacture the main structure of the aircraft such as the fuselage or wings, you need to build a two or three-story building.AutoclaveOr an autoclave, the pressurization process consumes a lot of time and money. If we can manufacture the main structural parts of the aircraft without an autoclave, we can get rid of all these huge equipment. "

         Since 2015, post-doctoral Jeonyoo Lee, Wardle laboratory members, and aerospace structure monitoring company Metis Design have formed a research team to create a method for manufacturing aerospace-grade composite materials without using a heating furnace. The researchers did not place the material layers in a heating furnace for curing, but wrapped them in an ultra-thin carbon nanotube film. When the film is energized, the carbon nanotubes, like a nano-scale electric blanket, quickly generate heat, so that the materials in it solidify and fuse together. Using this "Out of Oven process" (OoO), the research team can complete the production of aerospace-grade composite materials with only 1% of energy, and its strength is equivalent to that of composite materials made by traditional manufacturing methods.



Comparison of energy consumed by OoO method and traditional method

 


       "Each layer of the material has a microscopic surface roughness. When the two layers of material are put together, air will be trapped between the rough areas. This is the main source of pores and defects in the composite material. Using large-scale hot pressing The advantage of can making composite materials is that the autoclave can generate high enough pressure to extrude and eliminate any pores or air pockets between the materials from the edge of the interface." Wardle explained.

       Researchers including the Wardle laboratory research group have conducted long-term exploration in the field of "Out of Autoclave" (OoA), which allows composite materials to be manufactured without the use of large autoclaves. However, nearly 1% of the materials contain pores, which will affect the strength and life of the material. In contrast, aerospace-grade composite materials manufactured by the traditional autoclave method are of high quality, and any pores contained are negligible and difficult to measure.

        The problem with the non-autoclave process is that the materials are specially formulated, and there is no material suitable for main bearing structures such as wings and fuselage. Although some progress has been made in secondary load-bearing structural parts (such as flaps and doors), the product still has porosity.

       Part of the researchers’ work is focused on the development of nanoporous networks, which are ultra-thin films made of uniformly oriented microscopic materials (such as carbon nanotubes) that can be specially designed including color, strength, and capacitance. Researchers want to know whether these nanoporous films can replace large autoclaves to eliminate interlayer void defects.

       According to the researchers’ hypothesis, if a thin film of carbon nanotubes is placed between two layers of materials, as the material heats and softens, the space between the carbon nanotubes will have a capillary effect due to the additional pressure formed by the geometry and surface energy. , So that the materials attract each other instead of leaving pores between them. According to Lee's calculation, the capillary pressure should be greater than the pressure applied by the autoclave.


       In laboratory research and testing, the researchers first developed a growth technology for vertically aligned carbon nanotube films, and then placed the film between the layers of materials commonly used to prepare the main bearing structure of aircraft by the autoclave method. Then, these newly composed multilayer materials are wrapped in the second layer of carbon nanotube film, and the carbon nanotube film is energized to heat it. They observed that, as expected, as the material heats and softens, they are pulled into the capillary of the carbon nanotube film. The cross-sectional view of the composite material shows that,Nanoporous films with morphologically controllable nanoscale capillaries provide material preparationComposite materialRequired pressure,There are no voids between the resulting composite layers.


       The researchers conducted a strength test on the composite material, and they found that the composite material made by the new process is as strong as the composite material made by the traditional standard autoclave process. They used a sample several centimeters wide in the experiment, and the size of the sample is sufficient to prove that the nanopore network can pressurize the material and prevent the formation of voids. In order for the process to be used to make entire wings and fuselages, researchers will have to find a way to make larger porous films of carbon nanotubes.

       The researchers plan to explore different formulations in the future to obtain capillary nanoporous films with different surface energies and geometries suitable for engineering, so that they can be applied to other high-performance materials.

       The research was supported by MIT’s Nano Engineering Composites Aerospace Structure Consortium (NECST). Some supporting companies include Airbus, ANSYS, Embraer, Lockheed Martin, Saab AB, SA Tex Corporation and Teijin America Corporation, etc.
